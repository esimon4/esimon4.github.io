Overview
Each semester, TroyLabs takes a cohort of five USC-founded startups and puts them through an 8-week accelerator program called BUILD. The start-ups enter BUILD with a raw MVP and leave ready to begin fundraising. Across those 8-weeks, we help founders refine their go-to market strategies, optimize their technology stacks, improve their UX and UI, and ensure their pitches are VC-ready.

I served as a UX/UI consultant in the Fall of 2021. Across the course of the semester, I would meet with each company in the accelerator (at least) once a week to discuss, review, and iterate on their product design and brand‚Äôs digital presence.

For companies developing software, this could mean macro adjustments such as re-thinking specific user journies or tweaking the UX of certain features. For other companies, particulary those developing physical products, my role often entailed re-tooling landing pages or altering the company‚Äôs brand identity.

Outside of design, I consulted on general business development, marketing strategies, and pitch approaches.
Takeaway #1 ‚Äî How to Critique & Speak Design
When consulting on design at TroyLabs, I was rarely ever in the driver‚Äôs seat. I wasn‚Äôt generating my own designs or mapping out my own user experiences. I was instead mostly critiquing existing digital experiences ‚Äî be it marketing websites or more complex web apps.

Design is a kind of intuitive skill ‚Äî often something looks or feels off and it‚Äôs hard to articulate why or how it could be improved. I wasn‚Äôt hopping on Figma myself to manually change anything, so I instead had to find the words to describe just why or how specific design choices didn‚Äôt make sense.

Describing a certain color scheme as ‚Äúoff‚Äù wasn‚Äôt enough. I instead had to articulate how that color scheme made me feel, why that feeling wasn‚Äôt cohesive with a company‚Äôs brand presence, and how it could be improved. I had to find examples on dribbble and Pinterest to illustrate effective uses of gradients and shadows. Consulting and critiquing design pushed me to develop the rhetorical arsenal to offer productive and effective feedback. It also taught me to be purposeful, specific, and concrete when collaborating with others on a design ‚Äî vauge adjectives didn‚Äôt cut it.

I also learned, especially when thinking about more macro UX issues, the about power of sketching ‚Äî no matter how crappy of an artist you may be. Some of the features I helped work on were complex and included dozens of steps and instances. It was always productive to take a step back and map out user journeys with a pad and pencil before jumping into suggestions on how to make things more accessible and easy-to-use.
Takeaway #2 ‚Äî Always Come Back to the User
One of the biggest mistakes I saw during my time at BUILD was companies prioritizing aesthetics at the expense of usability. I found companies often opting for complex animations and user interactivity that obscured the reachability of end transactions ‚Äî such as purchasing a product, signing up for a newsletter, or applying for a waitlist.

I found it helpful to encourage founders to think about the different types of users who would be visting their website or using their product. What would they be hoping to do once they log on? From there, I helped different companies think about and flesh out 2-3 distinct user profiles ‚Äî who the users are and what they want to do.

In the end, it was helpful to land on 2-3 primary end transactions that each distinct user may desire. Once we decided those transactions, I emphasized the importance that they should always be at most 3 clicks away from any page or location on a site or app. If users had to click more than 3 times, the transaction was inacessible.

I found this concrete approach effective to help founders re-think their approach to UX and consistently come back to the user when designing.
